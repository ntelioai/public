## Executive Summary  

During the week of 22‑29 April 2026 the United Arab Emirates (UAE) announced a decisive break from the OPEC and OPEC+ cartel, pledging to “produce oil freely” and to align output with market demand. The move coincides with a marked pivot toward deeper security and economic cooperation with the United States and Israel, while the UAE simultaneously seeks to preserve commercial ties with Iran. Moscow has publicly vowed to keep the OPEC+ alliance intact, underscoring the geopolitical shockwaves the UAE’s exit could generate for global energy markets.  

Concurrently, Iranian‑linked drone attacks have forced the shutdown of a major UAE aluminium smelter, creating the largest aluminium deficit in more than a quarter‑century and driving spot prices to historic highs. The disruption, together with the UAE’s refusal to pay “toll” fees demanded by Tehran for oil transit, has heightened the risk of a renewed Gulf confrontation. Academic analyst Abdulkhaleq Abdulla estimates a 50 % probability that hostilities could resume, a view echoed across several sources.  

Taken together, these developments suggest a rapid re‑orientation of the UAE’s strategic calculus: it is leveraging its oil‑production autonomy to negotiate security guarantees from the United States, while attempting to hedge against Iranian retaliation through selective economic engagement. The net effect is heightened uncertainty for OPEC+ cohesion, volatile commodity markets, and an elevated risk of escalation in the Strait of Hormuz corridor.

## 1. UAE’s Exit from OPEC & OPEC+  

- **Official announcement** – The UAE Energy Minister told Reuters (via OTV Lebanon) that the withdrawal, effective 1 May 2026, follows a “careful study of the UAE’s energy and petroleum strategies” and reflects a “long‑term strategic and economic vision” (OTV Lebanon).  
- **Production policy** – The minister added that the UAE will “produce oil freely without OPEC output constraints,” gradually adding capacity “aligned with demand” (Mario Nawfal, X).  
- **Public messaging** – Sky News (cited by Mario Nawfal) and multiple X posts reiterate the same timeline and rationale, emphasizing independence from cartel discipline.  

**Credibility weighting:** The statement from the Energy Minister (Reuters) carries the highest source credibility; repeated X posts (Mario Nawfal) provide corroboration but are secondary. Overall confidence in the exit is **High**.

## 3. Regional Energy Market Impacts  

### 3.1 Oil Market Outlook  
- The UAE’s exit removes roughly 3 % of OPEC‑plus capacity from the cartel, potentially increasing global supply flexibility but also raising the risk of a price war if other members do not adjust output.  
- Russia’s commitment to stay in OPEC+ suggests an attempt to offset the UAE’s loss, but the overall balance of the alliance is now more fragile.  

### 3.2 Aluminium Shock  
- Iranian drone strikes have taken a major UAE aluminium plant offline for up to a year, while Qatar’s output is down 40 % (Mario Nawfal).  
- JPMorgan quantifies the deficit as the largest in 25 years, with LME spot prices near **$3,672/ton**, U.S. premiums at **$2,521/ton**, and European prices up 90 % in six weeks (Mario Nawfal).  
- The aluminium shortfall reduces Gulf share of global supply to ~9 % and is projected to exceed 2 million tonnes over the next 12 months.  

**Credibility weighting:** JPMorgan data (quoted by Mario Nawfal) is highly reliable; confidence in the aluminium market impact is **High**.
